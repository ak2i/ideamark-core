# 9. Projection Non-goals and Open Issues

**Version:** IdeaMark Core v1.2.0  
**Status:** Draft

## Purpose

This chapter defines the non-goals and open issues for Part 5.

Projection is central to IdeaMark use, but Part 5 must avoid turning Projection into a universal ontology, workflow engine, legal rule system, authoring engine, or retrieval algorithm.

## Non-goals

Part 5 does not define:

- a universal Projection serialization format;
- concrete YAML fields for IdeaMark documents;
- a universal prompt format;
- a universal domain ontology;
- a universal legal or organizational taxonomy;
- a universal authority ranking system;
- a required retrieval algorithm;
- a required vector search model;
- a required matching score;
- a required user interface;
- a required governance process;
- a required Projection Library implementation;
- a required storage model;
- a required AI model or agent architecture;
- a required progressive authoring engine;
- the Progressive Occurrence Resolution algorithm;
- a complete theory of meaning.

These may be defined by Part 4, companion specifications, tool contracts, domain profiles, Projection Libraries, or implementations.

## Not a Prompt Specification

A Projection may be expressed inside a prompt, generated by a prompt, or used to generate prompts.

However, Projection is not equivalent to prompt text.

Prompt text is often local, transient, and execution-oriented.

Projection is intended to be reusable, inspectable, comparable, and versionable.

A prompt may carry a Projection poorly or well.

Part 5 defines the Projection responsibilities, not a universal prompting style.

## Not a Schema for Meaning

Projection does not define final meaning.

It guides access, decomposition, matching, and reconstruction.

It may contain assumptions and requirements that strongly shape future meaning activation, but it does not replace the Situation, interpreter, Original Source, or activity.

## Not an Algorithm

Part 5 does not require a particular matching algorithm, retrieval algorithm, or authoring algorithm.

A simple implementation may use explicit metadata and human review.

A larger implementation may use indexes, embeddings, graph search, ranking models, rule engines, progressive authoring engines, or hybrid systems.

Conformance to Part 5 should depend on whether the Projection responsibilities are represented and usable, not on whether a specific algorithm is used.

## Not a Governance Mandate

Part 5 allows private, shared, organizational, public, experimental, deprecated, and standardized Projections.

It does not require every Projection to be approved by a central authority.

Governance is important for shared and high-risk use, but governance style belongs to organizations, domains, communities, or companion specifications.

## Not Progressive Occurrence Resolution

Progressive Occurrence Resolution, abbreviated as POR in the IdeaMark ecosystem, is outside the scope of Part 5.

POR is a stateful progressive authoring mechanism for large, streaming, composite, or difficult Original Sources.

It may use Projection as input, and it may emit IdeaMark-compatible structures as output.

However, POR's algorithm, internal intermediate representation, session state, LLM orchestration, and backward reinterpretation process belong to the IdeaMark-POR repository or another companion specification.

Part 5 should reserve POR for Progressive Occurrence Resolution and should not use POR to abbreviate Projection lifecycle or reuse concepts.

## Open Issue: Concrete Serialization

Part 5 currently defines conceptual and specification-side responsibilities.

A future Part 4 or companion Projection Profile may define concrete serialization fields such as:

- projection id;
- version;
- status;
- requirement bundle;
- source coverage policy;
- Section strategy;
- Entity strategy;
- Occurrence strategy;
- traceability strategy;
- retrieval intent;
- matching criteria;
- reconstruction targets;
- evaluation checklist;
- lifecycle metadata.

The open question is whether these should live inside IdeaMark document metadata, separate Projection documents, inline Projection fragments, or all three.

## Open Issue: Projection Compatibility Model

Part 5 identifies compatibility as a central concern.

However, the exact compatibility model remains open.

Possible approaches include:

- rule-based compatibility;
- declared compatibility ranges;
- profile inheritance;
- transformation declarations;
- embedding-assisted retrieval;
- human-reviewed compatibility;
- library-maintained compatibility tables;
- hybrid approaches.

A future companion specification may define a minimal compatibility vocabulary.

## Open Issue: Projection Quality Evaluation

Projection quality may be evaluated by different criteria depending on use.

Possible dimensions include:

- interpretation cost reduction;
- retrieval success;
- reconstruction usefulness;
- traceability sufficiency;
- mismatch avoidance;
- human review burden;
- transferability;
- explainability;
- safety;
- maintainability;
- library reuse frequency.

Part 5 should not freeze a universal quality score too early.

## Open Issue: Projection Engineering

Projection Engineering refers to the design, evaluation, improvement, comparison, and lifecycle management of Projections.

This is distinct from Progressive Occurrence Resolution.

Future work should clarify:

- how candidate Projections are proposed;
- how humans review them;
- how feedback updates them;
- how Projection Libraries retain successful strategies;
- how failed Projections are archived or deprecated;
- how Projection compatibility and evaluation evidence are preserved.

## Open Issue: Privacy-preserving Projection References

Some Projections may need to remain private while still being referenced by IdeaMark documents.

Future work should define how documents can record enough information for traceability and compatibility without disclosing sensitive internal requirements.

Possible mechanisms include:

- opaque Projection IDs;
- public summaries;
- private local resolution;
- signed metadata;
- capability-scoped access;
- compatibility attestations.

## Review Direction

Before Part 5 becomes stable, reviewers should check whether it:

- clearly separates Projection from Core;
- clearly separates Projection from Situation;
- clearly explains generation-side and use-side Projection;
- supports partial compatibility rather than exact identity only;
- preserves Original Source priority;
- gives enough guidance for Part 4 metadata design;
- avoids prescribing implementation algorithms prematurely;
- treats POR as Progressive Occurrence Resolution and keeps it outside Core;
- supports private, shared, and public Projection use.
